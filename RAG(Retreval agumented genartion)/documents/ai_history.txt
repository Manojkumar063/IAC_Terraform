Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a field of computer science focused on building systems capable of performing tasks that normally require human intelligence. These tasks include learning, reasoning, problem solving, perception, and language understanding.

The concept of artificial intelligence dates back to ancient history where myths described artificial beings with intelligence. However, the modern field of AI began in the 1950s.

In 1956, the Dartmouth Conference marked the official birth of AI as a research discipline. Researchers such as John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Allen Newell, and Herbert Simon believed that machines could be made to simulate human intelligence.

Early AI systems focused on symbolic reasoning and rule-based systems. These systems attempted to encode human knowledge using logical rules.

In the 1970s and 1980s, AI experienced periods known as "AI winters" where funding and interest declined due to limited progress.

In the 1990s, machine learning began to replace rule-based approaches. Instead of manually programming rules, systems began learning patterns from data.

In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov, marking a milestone in AI research.

In the 2000s, advances in computational power and large datasets enabled the rise of deep learning. Neural networks with many layers could now process complex patterns in images, speech, and text.

Today AI powers many technologies including recommendation systems, speech recognition, autonomous vehicles, and generative models such as large language models.

Large language models are trained on massive amounts of text data and can generate human-like responses. They are widely used in chatbots, content generation, and knowledge assistants.

AI continues to evolve rapidly, with research focusing on explainability, safety, fairness, and general intelligence.
